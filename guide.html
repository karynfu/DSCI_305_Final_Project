How to use
ClearConsent.

A step-by-step walkthrough for patients, caregivers, and research coordinators who want to turn a dense consent form into something they can actually understand.

Why you might use this

Most clinical trial consent forms are written at a college reading level and run 20+ pages. If you've been handed one and feel like you're missing what you're actually agreeing to, you're not alone. Research shows most trial participants can't accurately describe key parts of their own study after signing.

ClearConsent takes the form you were given and does four things:

  • Pulls out the key facts (condition, drug, trial phase, duration, visits, compensation).
  • Writes a plain-English summary at roughly a 6th-grade reading level.
  • Highlights red-flag clauses patients often miss (ex. data reuse, limited injury pay, unknown long-term risks).
  • Suggests questions to ask your doctor when you discuss enrollment.

Getting started

  1. Open ClearConsent in your browser.
  2. Have your consent form ready, either as a PDF file or as copied text from the document.
  3. Decide which input method to use. Both produce the same kind of analysis; pick whichever is more convenient.

Two ways to submit a form

Option A — Paste text

Fastest if you already have the text

Click the Paste text tab. Copy the full body of the consent form and paste it into the box. Then click Analyze consent form. If you want to try the tool without a real form, click Load a sample consent form →.

Option B — Upload PDF

Best if you only have the PDF

Click the Upload PDF tab, then click the dashed drop zone to pick your file. Make sure the PDF is downloaded to your device. Maximum file size is 10MB.

Reading your results

After a few seconds of loading, you'll see four cards. Here's what each one is for and how to use it.

1. Study at a Glance

A structured summary of the trial's core details: condition being studied, drug or intervention, trial phase, duration, number of visits, compensation, location, and principal investigator. If the form doesn't mention a field, you'll see Not specified rather than a made-up answer.

2. Plain-English Summary

A 3–5 sentence explanation of the study written at roughly a 6th-grade reading level. Covers what the trial is testing, what will happen to you as a participant, and the main risks involved. Good to read first if you only have a minute.

3. Red Flags to Be Aware Of

3–4 specific clauses that are worth re-reading closely; things like data being reused for future research without your consent, limited financial compensation if you're injured, unknown long-term risks, invasive procedures, or being randomly assigned to a placebo. These are not necessarily reasons to refuse the trial, but rather points you should make sure you understand before signing.

4. Questions to Ask Your Doctor

4–5 conversational, actionable questions tailored to your specific form. Bring these to your next appointment with the study team or your primary care provider.

Tips for the best results

  • Upload the full form, not an excerpt. The model's analysis is only as good as the content it sees. A one-page summary will produce a vaguer output than the complete document.
  • Use the PDF upload for scanned or formatted forms. Pasting sometimes drops tables and headers; uploading preserves the original layout.
  • Don't upload forms with your personal health information. The form is sent to Google's API. Stick to generic forms or redact patient-identifying details first.
  • Treat the output as a starting point, not a verdict. The red flags and questions are meant to trigger a better conversation with your care team — not replace one.

Troubleshooting

The page shows "Empty response from model"

This usually means the form is unusually long and the model ran out of space to write its answer. Try splitting the form and analyzing each section separately, or use the paste option with just the key sections (purpose, procedures, risks, compensation, confidentiality).

The page shows an API key error

The Gemini API key has either expired, been rotated, or hit its daily free-tier quota. If you're running the tool yourself, get a new key from Google AI Studio and paste it into the top of the <script> block in index.html.

The output mentions details my form doesn't contain

Re-check that you uploaded the correct file or pasted the full text. If the form is extremely short or cut off, the model may fill gaps with plausible-sounding defaults. The prompt is tuned to use Not specified instead, but this can still happen — if you see something that looks invented, trust the original document.
